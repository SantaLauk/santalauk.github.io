Europe's approach to cybersecurity has, for years, moved at the pace of policy: measured, 
considered, shaped by committees and review cycles. That is not a criticism. It reflects 
how the region prefers to govern technology, carefully and with broad consensus. The 
difficulty is that the threats this framework is designed to address operate on a very 
different timeline. 

Artificial intelligence has sharpened that tension. It is not a new presence in cybersecurity. 
Defenders have used AI-assisted tools for well over a decade, primarily for anomaly 
detection and alert filtering. What has changed is the degree to which the same 
capabilities are now available to attackers, and the effect that has had on the speed, scale, 
and sophistication of the threats European organisations are dealing with. 

## A Threat Landscape That No Longer Looks Familiar 

The European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) published its 2025 Threat 
Landscape report in October, covering 4,875 incidents recorded over a twelve-month 
period. The overall picture was not simply one of more attacks. It was of an ecosystem that 
has become more organised, more interconnected, and more difficult to attribute to any 
single actor or motive. 

Ransomware remains the most financially damaging category of threat across the EU. 
What has shifted is how criminal groups structure their operations. Faced with sustained 
law enforcement pressure, they have decentralised: infrastructure is spread across 
jurisdictions, independent groups run attacks under shared brand names, and 
ransomware is made available as a service to attackers with no technical background of 
their own. The model has become remarkably resilient to disruption. 

Phishing continues to be the primary entry point, accounting for roughly 60% of observed 
cases. The tactics, however, have changed considerably. According to ENISA's findings, 
over 80% of phishing emails identified over a recent six-month period leveraged artificial 
intelligence to some extent. Messages have become personalised and contextually 
convincing, constructed from scraped professional data and refined to avoid the signals 
that most users have learned to look for. The poorly written email from an implausible 
address is largely extinct. 

The report also flagged the emergence of tools specifically designed to support criminal 
operations, including malicious AI systems such as Xanthorox AI, built to automate social 
engineering and malware development, alongside fraudulent websites impersonating 
legitimate AI platforms to distribute malware. Attackers are not only adopting AI as a tool, 
they are beginning to build infrastructure around it. 

## The Regulatory Bind 

Europe's instinct when faced with a technology that carries significant risk is to regulate it. 
The EU AI Act, the first comprehensive legal framework for AI anywhere in the world, 
reflects that instinct. It began applying its strictest prohibitions in February 2025 and its 
rules for general-purpose AI models took effect in August of the same year. It is, by any 
measure, a serious piece of legislation. 

For cybersecurity specifically, though, it creates a tension that has not yet been fully 
resolved. AI systems used for threat detection, risk scoring, or access control are likely to 
fall into the Act's high-risk category, triggering requirements on transparency, human 
oversight, and ongoing conformity assessments. Those obligations are not unreasonable. 
The difficulty is that they fall on legitimate vendors operating within the EU, while the 
criminal groups and state-sponsored actors they are building defences against are subject 
to none of them. 

Security work depends on speed. Identifying a new attack technique, updating a detection 
model, sharing intelligence: these things need to happen quickly, often within hours of a 
threat being identified. Compliance processes are not designed for that pace, and there is a 
genuine concern that requirements built for high-risk AI in general have not been 
sufficiently adapted for what high-risk means in an operational security context. 

The AI Act does not sit alone. GDPR, NIS2, the Cyber Resilience Act, and a range of 
sector-specific frameworks together create a compliance environment that is particularly 
demanding for smaller vendors and mid-sized organisations. Each regulation has a 
legitimate purpose. Navigating all of them simultaneously requires resources that many of 
the companies most in need of strong security tools simply do not have. 

## The Gaps That Regulation Cannot Fill 

Not all of Europe's cybersecurity vulnerabilities are regulatory in origin. Several are 
structural, and they predate the current conversation about AI by a considerable margin. 

The skills shortage is the most persistent. ENISA estimates the gap within the EU at 
299,000 professionals, a figure that grew 9% in a single year. University and vocational 
pipelines have not kept pace with demand, and public sector organisations, which are 
frequently among the most targeted, struggle most to retain qualified staff. The World 
Economic Forum’s Global Cybersecurity Outlook 2025 found that 49% of public-sector 
organisations say they lack the talent to meet their cybersecurity goals — up 33% from the 
previous year.  

Capacity is also unevenly distributed across member states. In its first Report on the State 
of Cybersecurity in the Union, ENISA highlighted that cybersecurity maturity remains 
notably fragmented across member states. While countries with long-established national 
frameworks possess mature national agencies and a developed private security sector, 
other parts of the bloc face severe resource constraints. EU legislation sets a common 
standard, but it does not create common capacity, and the weakest points in its defences 
are not difficult to identify. 

Supply chain risk has emerged as a particular concern, with third-party compromises cited 
as the second most significant cybersecurity threat by European organisations looking 
ahead. ENISA's 2025 report documented multiple cases where attackers targeted external 
IT service providers to compromise downstream clients. In early 2025, a breach of an 
external provider managing the Telemaco platform for multiple Italian transport 
companies paralysed ticketing systems for two days, impacting several thousand 
commuters. Similar supply chain incidents affected Berlin's public transport operator 
BVG, with customer data for 180,000 passengers exposed via an external provider, and 
Spain's Repsol, whose customer data was compromised through a third-party vendor. As 
reliance on outsourced services grows, so does the attack surface that comes with it. 

Mobile devices represent a further exposure, accounting for more than 42% of observed 
threats in ENISA's 2025 dataset. Android devices were particularly targeted, with remote 
access tools used for both financial fraud and espionage. Given that mobile devices are 
now routinely used for corporate authentication and access to sensitive systems, often 
under less rigorous security configurations than fixed enterprise devices, this remains an 
area where the gap between risk and practice is wide. 

## What a Credible Response Looks Like 

Discussions about cybersecurity tend to move between two unhelpful extremes. Either the 
scale of the problem is framed in terms that make any response feel inadequate, or the 
focus narrows to compliance requirements that can be met without any meaningful 
improvement in actual security. Neither framing produces useful outcomes. 

The evidence consistently points to the same starting point: most successful attacks in 
Europe exploit known vulnerabilities with available fixes. Yet ENISA's NIS Investments 
2025 report found that 28% of organisations take more than three months to apply critical 
patches, and 30% had not conducted any cybersecurity assessment in the previous twelve 
months. Prompt patching, multi-factor authentication, access controls built around 
genuine need, and staff training on social engineering are not sophisticated interventions, 
but organisations that apply them rigorously are substantially harder to compromise. The 
basics still matter, and in many cases they are still not being done. 

Cross-border intelligence sharing is an area where the EU has the right structures but is 
still developing the pace those foundations require. ENISA's coordination role, the NIS2 
framework, and the European Cyber Shield initiative all provide mechanisms for sharing 
threat intelligence across member states. In practice, that intelligence often moves 
through institutional channels that were not designed to operate at the speed of an active 
threat. ENISA’s NIS Investments 2025 report found that at least one in two organisations 
in sectors newly brought into scope under NIS2 do not participate in any information
sharing initiative. Closing that gap is achievable and would have a tangible impact. 

The regulatory framework needs to develop a more precise understanding of how its 
requirements apply to security-specific AI tools. The AI Act represents a genuine 
achievement in terms of establishing common rules for a technology that had none. But its 
application to cybersecurity cannot simply be read across from other sectors. The people 
who build and operate these tools need to be central to that conversation as the framework 
continues to evolve. 

And the investment in people needs to be treated as infrastructure, not as a training 
budget line. A shortage of 299,000 professionals does not close through university 
diploma alone. It requires sustained attention to vocational pathways, mid-career 
transitions into the field, and public sector conditions that do not make experienced 
professionals feel like they are making a financial sacrifice to work for the institutions 
most in need of protection. 

## Where This Leaves Europe 

Europe is not without resources or ambition on cybersecurity. The regulatory frameworks 
it has built are serious, the institutions it has established are capable, and the awareness of 
the problem at a political level is higher than it has ever been. The challenge is translating 
that into operational effectiveness at the speed the current threat environment demands. 

Criminal ecosystems are continuing to professionalise. State-linked actors are testing 
critical infrastructure with greater regularity. AI is reducing the skill threshold required to 
carry out sophisticated attacks and raising the ceiling of what those attacks can achieve. 
These trends are not new, but they are accelerating. 

Europe has built the architecture. The question now is whether it can make it work fast 
enough to matter.
